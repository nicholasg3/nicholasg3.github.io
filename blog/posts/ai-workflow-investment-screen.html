The AI Workflow Investment Screen

Before buying another tool, decide what kind of work you are willing to change.

The quickest way to make an AI plan sound serious is to give every department a pilot. Sales gets account research. Support gets summarisation. Finance gets invoice checks. Product gets discovery notes. Nobody wants to be the person saying no, so the list grows.

The list is usually the problem. It lets a company confuse activity with judgment. A pilot is not automatically a strategy because a model is involved, and a workflow is not automatically worth changing because someone can imagine a chatbot sitting inside it.

The bottleneck is rarely model access now. It is attention, ownership, review capacity, and tolerance for operational mess. Once a company starts using AI in real work, the hard questions become embarrassingly ordinary. Who checks the output? What happens when it is wrong? Which habit changes? Who is allowed to trust it? What do we stop doing if this works?

If the workflow is not worth changing, it is not worth automating.

That sounds obvious. In practice, it is the sentence that kills half the roadmap.

The first screen

I would not begin with model choice. I would begin with four questions, asked in plain language.

  1. What decision gets better? Not "productivity". Not "efficiency". Name the decision: which account to pursue, which ticket to escalate, which claim to make, which exception to approve.
  2. Is the work repeated enough to learn from? One-off executive judgment is usually a bad first target. Repeated work with a visible review path is much easier to improve.
  3. Does the company have context a generic model lacks? If not, the project may still save time, but it is probably not a strategic asset.
  4. Where does human responsibility remain? The answer should be specific enough that someone could use it on a Tuesday morning.

This is not a grand framework. It is a way to slow the room down before the demo takes over.

What AI changes

Current models are good enough to matter. They can draft, classify, retrieve, summarise, compare documents, call tools, and carry out narrow multi-step procedures. In many white-collar workflows, that is already enough to change the economics of the task.

But they do not remove the management problem. A model can prepare a sales brief. It cannot decide what kind of customer the company wants. It can summarise a refund policy. It cannot decide when keeping a furious customer is worth bending the rule. It can assemble pricing evidence. It cannot decide what a price is meant to say about the company.

So the useful question is not "Can AI do this?" Often it can do some version of it. The useful question is: where, exactly, should it sit in the chain of responsibility?

Three ordinary cases

Take account research. Before a sales call, a team wants company news, funding history, product signals, executive changes, and likely objections. AI can help. But a generic brief full of visible facts is not the prize. The real edge is joining those facts to the company's own memory: which triggers have actually led to revenue, which objections matter, which accounts look impressive but never close. That is an assisted workflow, not an autonomous one.

Support policy is different. Many tickets are repeated, bounded, and reviewable. AI can retrieve the policy and draft the answer. But the exception path matters more than the first answer. Refund discretion, security issues, legal exposure, and angry high-value customers should not be smoothed over by a fluent paragraph. This can be automated narrowly, if escalation is designed first.

Pricing is different again. It is tempting because the leverage is obvious. But pricing is not just analysis. It is positioning, margin, sales motion, procurement friction, and willingness to lose certain customers. AI can collect inputs and draft scenarios. It should not be treated as the owner of the recommendation unless the company is comfortable hiding a strategic sacrifice inside a spreadsheet-shaped answer.

The boundary most teams blur

There are at least four separate permissions: produce an output, recommend an action, execute the action, and learn from the result. Demos make these look like one smooth path. They are not.

Drafting a customer reply is one risk. Sending it is another. Applying a refund is another. Updating future policy because the refund "worked" is another again. Most AI governance problems start when those boundaries are left implicit.

Boundary Executive question Common control
Output Can AI draft, retrieve, classify, or compare safely? Source citation, formatting rules, human review.
Recommendation Can AI rank options without hiding the assumptions? Confidence bands, counter-arguments, named owner.
Execution Can AI act without creating unrecoverable harm? Approval thresholds, escalation rules, audit trail.
Learning Can the system improve without drifting from doctrine? Feedback cadence, dataset review, kill rule.

The three calls

After that, the decision is usually one of three things.

  • Automate when the work is repeated, bounded, low-harm, easy to review, and owned by someone with authority to change the process.
  • Assist when the workflow has high value but judgment, taste, risk, or relationship context must remain explicit.
  • Ignore when there is weak context advantage, poor adoption path, expensive error, or no named business decision.
Workflow Likely call Reason
Sales account research Assist It improves preparation speed, but a human still owns judgment and relationship context.
Customer support policy lookup Automate narrowly The work is repeated, bounded, reviewable, and can escalate exceptions.
Strategic pricing recommendation Ignore or assist only The consequence of a confident error is high and the context is commercially sensitive.

A quick test before the pilot

Before approving the next AI pilot, ask for one paragraph that names five things:

  • the business decision the workflow is meant to improve;
  • the person who owns the changed workflow;
  • the boundary between drafting, recommending, and acting;
  • the evidence that would count as a real improvement;
  • the condition under which the company will stop.

If the team cannot write that paragraph, it is probably too early to buy the tool. The delay may feel conservative. It is usually faster than spending a quarter discovering that the company automated a task nobody had really agreed to change.
