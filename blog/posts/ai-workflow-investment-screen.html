The AI Workflow Investment Screen

Buy tools only after you decide which work is worth redesigning and who owns the result.

Companies often treat generative AI spending as a capability race: which model scores highest, which vendor demo looks best, which feature list is longest. The tighter constraint is usually organizational. Gains depend on how well a tool fits the work people actually do, and on who remains accountable after the tool is installed. Information systems research made that point long before large language models. Task-technology fit research in the 1990s showed that performance tracks the match between technology and task, not technical capacity alone. The same logic applies here.

This memo proposes an investment screen for AI workflow proposals. The phrase means a selection filter, as in credit screening or investment screening. Before money is spent, each proposal should answer two questions in writing. Is the underlying work worth redesigning? If so, how much of it should an AI system perform? The working rule is narrow:

If the workflow is not worth changing, it is not worth automating.

What follows turns that rule into a procedure. It defines three labels for machine involvement, four questions to answer before product comparison, and four control points that separate drafting from recommending, acting, and learning. The goal is not delay for its own sake. It is to stop tool purchase from outrunning process design and accountability.

Why the screen is needed

The screen is needed because organizations often buy tools before they specify the work. When a firm decides it needs an AI strategy, the first artifact is often a wishlist of tasks people would rather not do by hand. Sales may want faster account research. Support may want help with ticket summaries. Finance may want less manual invoice checking. Details vary by department. The pattern does not: requests are collected, the collection is called a roadmap, and shopping starts before anyone agrees to redesign the work the tools would touch.

The screen is operational. A changed process needs a named owner, a review step that can catch plausible errors, and a plan to retire the old habit once a pilot is live. Adding a new step without removing an old one only increases workload. Vague ownership pushes that extra work onto whoever inherits the process later.

Three labels: automate, assist, ignore

Teams also need shared words for how much control an AI system is allowed over a task. Three labels are enough for a first cut: automate, assist, and ignore. They do not replace the investment screen. They only describe the degree of machine involvement under consideration.

  • Automate means the system does the task end to end; a person intervenes only on exceptions. Use it when the task is narrow, repeated, reviewable after the fact, and cheap to correct.
  • Assist means the system prepares material (a draft, a shortlist, a retrieved policy) while a person still owns the judgment call.
  • Ignore means leave the work alone for now. That is the right call when the process is undefined, the data are thin, nobody owns the outcome, or a wrong answer is costly.

As a first sort, the three labels are useful. As a whole strategy they are too forgiving, because almost any project can be relabeled "assist" and made to sound prudent. Nothing about the label costs anything to apply. What the sort leaves out is the running cost after the label is chosen. Review takes hours and managerial attention, and colleagues still have to trust prose they did not write. Putting a workflow in a bucket is cheaper than showing that the ongoing cost of running it is justified.

Four questions in the investment screen

The investment screen is four questions. Answer them in writing before the organization compares foundation-model APIs, evaluates vendor products, or schedules a product demonstration. The automate/assist/ignore labels only describe machine involvement. They do not show that the work is specified well enough to change. These questions do.

  1. What decision improves? Name the decision the workflow is supposed to improve (for example: which account to pursue, which ticket to escalate, which claim to file, which exception to approve).
  2. Does the work repeat? If the task does not recur, the organization will have little chance to compare outcomes or correct course across instances.
  3. What does the company know that a generic AI tool would not? If the honest answer is "nothing," the project may still save time, but it is unlikely to produce an advantage over competitors who can buy the same tool.
  4. Where does responsibility stay human? Name the role that signs off, escalates, or is accountable when the output is wrong. The answer should be specific enough that no second meeting is required to interpret it.

Demonstrations and pilots serve different purposes. Both are more useful after the four answers exist. A demonstration shows whether a tool can perform a task under selected conditions. A pilot runs the tool on the organization's own cases long enough to show who approves outputs in practice, and what the organization does when the system is wrong with high confidence on a consequential case.

Background: fit, entanglement, and displaced labor

The investment screen is not a new theory. It turns three recurring findings from information technology research into a buying checklist: task-technology fit, the entanglement of automation with augmentation, and the shift of interpretive labor onto recipients.

Dale Goodhue and Ronald Thompson (1995) established the baseline. Gains depend on how well a technology matches the task people are trying to do, not only on what the technology can do in isolation. For generative AI, a high-benchmark model aimed at an ill-specified workflow mainly produces activity. The screen's first two questions (what decision improves, and whether the work repeats) are fit questions. They come before model comparison because fit is a property of the work process, not of a benchmark table.

Fit also changes after adoption. Sebastian Raisch and Sebastian Krakowski (2021) show that automation and augmentation stay entangled over time. A system introduced as an assistant can absorb judgment that was never formally transferred. A system introduced to automate can need more supervision than the deployment plan assumed. That is why a one-time label of "assist" or "automate" is not enough, why the screen's fourth question asks where responsibility stays human, and why that answer must remain checkable after launch. Mohammad Hossein Jarrahi (2018) adds the division-of-labor point: machines scale analytical processing; people still supply context, tolerance for ambiguity, values, and situational judgment that do not reduce cleanly to a prompt. Leave that division implicit and the organization rediscovers it as review load.

That rediscovery has a popular name. Harvard Business Review and BetterUp use "workslop" for AI-generated material that looks finished while shifting interpretive work onto the recipient: what the text means, whether it is correct, what to do next. In fit terms, workslop is a tool-task mismatch that shows up as review work. In Raisch and Krakowski's terms, it is entanglement made routine: the system drafts, and people retake judgment without recording the transfer. The screen is meant to make that labor cost visible before the organization treats it as normal. Ethan Mollick's Co-Intelligence is strongest on day-to-day collaboration with uneven model abilities. Mustafa Suleyman's The Coming Wave is strongest on containment and governance. Both support the same practice: treat capability and control as separate decisions, and only after the work itself is specified.

What AI changes in ordinary work

For ordinary office AI, the investment question is where the tool sits in the chain of responsibility once it is used every day. Whether models can draft, retrieve, summarize, or run narrow procedures is largely settled. On many routine tasks they can, and that already changes the cost of those tasks.

Take a sales brief. A model can produce the brief quickly. The judgment about which kind of customer the company wants is not in that brief; it remains a human decision about pursuit. Refunds and pricing show the same split. Policy text is easy to summarize; deciding when to break policy for a long-time customer is not. Pricing inputs can be assembled quickly, but what a price is meant to signal about the company still has to be owned. Capability is usually easier to establish than accountability. Accountability placement decides whether the tool should be bought and how it should be governed.

Three ordinary cases

Three common commercial workflows show how the same screen produces different decisions. Account research is usually assist. Support policy lookup can often be automated narrowly. Strategic pricing should usually stay ignore or assist-only. Each case meets the four questions differently.

Account research before a sales call needs company news, funding history, product signals, executive changes, and likely objections. AI can assemble public material into a brief. Any real advantage usually comes from proprietary knowledge: which triggers have historically led to revenue, which objections kill deals, which impressive accounts never close. Those judgments should stay with a person who knows that history. The system can prepare the brief. It should not own the pursuit decision.

For support policy automation, the main risk is the exception path, not the average ticket. Most tickets are repeated, bounded, and easy to check after the fact, so an AI system can retrieve the policy and draft a reply on mainstream work. Exceptions differ in kind. A refund commits money. A security or legal ticket can create liability. A high-value or long-tenure customer often requires judgment about tone and discretion that is not in the policy text. If those cases are not routed to a person before the system answers, the organization finds the failure only after the reply or payment has gone out. Narrow automation is justified only when the mainstream path is automated and the exception path is specified in advance: what counts as an exception, who receives it, and what the system may not do while waiting.

Strategic pricing fails the screen more often because it is easy to mistake for a calculation problem. Pricing work often involves competitor tables, discount histories, margin math, and scenario spreadsheets. Those materials look like analytical work software handles well, so teams are often tempted to hand the recommendation to a model. The screen focuses on another aspect of pricing. Pricing is also a choice about market position, which customers the company is willing to lose, how sales will sell the number, and what procurement friction the firm will accept. An AI system can gather inputs and draft scenarios for those choices. Letting it produce the recommendation risks treating a strategic tradeoff as if a completed table had already resolved it.

Four control points in an AI workflow

An AI workflow usually contains at least four permissions that product demos treat as one feature: producing an output, recommending an action, taking the action, and learning from the outcome. Those are separate control points. Governance fails when an organization approves them with a single decision. A product demonstration can present them as one continuous step. In production they separate again, and each layer can fail on its own.

A customer refund shows why the four permissions must be governed separately. Drafting a reply is an output decision. Sending the reply places the company's voice on record. Issuing money is execution. Adjusting policy because a refund "worked" is learning: it rewrites the rule for later cases, and it is the step least often watched. If a system executes and learns without explicit controls, a manager's sign-off on a recommendation is weak control. Governance has to cover each permission, not only the draft.

Control point Question for the owner Common control
Output Can AI draft, retrieve, classify, or compare safely? Source citation, formatting rules, human review.
Recommendation Can AI rank options without hiding the assumptions? Confidence bands, counter-arguments, named owner.
Execution Can AI act without creating unrecoverable harm? Approval thresholds, escalation rules, audit trail.
Learning Can the system improve without changing policy unless a person reviews the change? Feedback cadence, dataset review, kill rule.

Use the label at the end, not the start

Apply automate, assist, or ignore after the four questions are answered, not before. Used that way, the label is an operating choice with consequences for staffing, review, and accountability. It is not a decorative taxonomy on a vendor slide.

  • Automate the repeated work, inside a narrow scope, with an exception path defined in advance.
  • Assist the valuable work where judgment, taste, risk, or relationship context needs to stay visible to a person.
  • Ignore the work with weak context, unclear ownership, costly failure modes, or no named decision behind it.
Workflow Likely call Reason
Sales account research Assist It improves preparation speed, but a human still owns judgment and relationship context.
Customer support policy lookup Automate narrowly The work is repeated, bounded, reviewable, and can escalate exceptions.
Strategic pricing recommendation Ignore or assist only The consequence of a confident error is high and the context is commercially sensitive.

A quick test before the pilot

A short written test is enough to decide whether a pilot is premature. Before approving the next AI pilot, ask the team for one paragraph that names five things:

  • the business decision the workflow is meant to improve;
  • the person who owns the changed workflow;
  • the separation between drafting, recommending, and acting;
  • the evidence that would count as a real improvement;
  • the condition under which the company will stop.

If the team cannot write that paragraph, the tool is probably premature, regardless of what a demonstration showed. Delaying a purchase can feel slow relative to a vendor timeline. In practice it is often faster than spending a quarter learning that the organization automated a task nobody had agreed to redesign.

Conclusion

The claim is limited and practical. Generative AI can change the cost of ordinary office work. Whether that change is a good investment depends less on model benchmarks than on three organizational conditions: whether the tool fits the task, whether someone owns the redesigned process, and which permissions the system may exercise. The investment screen checks those conditions before product comparison begins.

The procedure has three steps. First, answer the four questions in writing: decision, repetition, proprietary knowledge, human responsibility. Second, apply automate, assist, or ignore only after those answers exist. Third, treat output, recommendation, execution, and learning as separate control points, each with an owner and a control. If a team cannot name the decision, the owner, the control points, the success evidence, and the stop condition, a pilot is premature.

None of this requires a new theory of AI. It requires the same discipline used for other capital decisions: specify the work, assign accountability, and buy the tool only when both are clear. The working rule at the start of the memo is also the right place to stop:

If the workflow is not worth changing, it is not worth automating.
