The AI Workflow Investment Screen

Before buying another tool, decide what kind of work you are willing to change.

The meeting usually begins with a list. A neat list. A responsible list. Sales wants account research. Support wants ticket summaries. Finance wants invoice checks. Product wants customer-interview notes. Someone has seen a demo. Someone else has a vendor deck. Around the table there is that small professional pressure to appear modern, alert, unsentimental about change.

Then the list gets longer. Nobody wants to be the person with the small imagination. Nobody wants to say that the magic box might create a new review queue, a new compliance headache, a new set of half-trusted drafts sitting in Slack at 6:42 p.m. So the company calls the list a roadmap.

A roadmap is generous. Often it is just a catalogue of anxieties with software attached.

If the workflow is not worth changing, it is not worth automating.

That sentence sounds like common sense. In a real planning session, it is a little bomb under the table. It forces everyone to ask the dull, adult questions. Who owns the changed process? Who reviews the machine's work? What happens when the answer is plausible and wrong? Which old habit disappears if the pilot succeeds?

Start with automate, assist, or ignore

The automate / assist / ignore frame is useful because it gives the room a common language before the conversation dissolves into vendor names.

  • Automate when the task is narrow, repeated, reviewable, and cheap to correct.
  • Assist when the machine can prepare the work, while a person still owns the judgment.
  • Ignore when the process is confused, the data is thin, the owner is missing, or the error would be expensive.

As a first sort, this is good. As a whole strategy, it is too forgiving. A team can call almost anything "assist" and feel prudent. The label sounds modest. The implementation still consumes attention, review time, trust, and managerial patience.

The first screen

Before model choice, ask four plain questions. They are almost embarrassingly simple, which is why they work.

  1. What decision improves? Name it. Which account to pursue. Which ticket to escalate. Which claim to make. Which exception to approve.
  2. Does the work repeat? Repetition gives the company a chance to compare, correct, and learn.
  3. What does the company know that the model does not? If the answer is "nothing", the project may still save time. It will rarely create an edge.
  4. Where does responsibility stay human? The answer should be clear enough for someone to use on an ordinary Tuesday morning.

The point is to slow the room down before the demo takes over. Demos are built to collapse distance. A serious pilot restores distance: between draft and decision, suggestion and action, confidence and accountability.

Where this argument comes from

This is an old information-systems problem wearing new clothes. Dale Goodhue and Ronald Thompson's work on task-technology fit made the basic point in the 1990s: performance improves when the technology fits the work people actually have to do. The same idea applies to generative AI. A powerful model aimed at the wrong task still produces expensive theatre.

Management scholars Sebastian Raisch and Sebastian Krakowski add a useful warning. Automation and augmentation are tangled over time. A system that begins as an assistant can gradually absorb judgment; a system designed for automation may require more human supervision than the vendor deck admits. The useful question is therefore less heroic than "replace or empower?" It is: which pieces of judgment move, and who notices when they move?

Mohammad Hossein Jarrahi's work on human-machine symbiosis points in the same direction. Machines are strong at analytical processing; people carry context, ambiguity, values, and practical sense. The better AI workflow is usually the one that names this division clearly, then tests whether it holds under pressure.

The recent business press has been circling the same problem from the shop floor. Harvard Business Review and BetterUp call one failure mode "workslop": AI-generated material that looks finished while pushing interpretive work onto someone else. The label is inelegant. The phenomenon is real. A workflow screen should catch this before it becomes normal office weather.

There is also a useful small bookshelf here. Ethan Mollick's Co-Intelligence is good on working with AI as a collaborator whose abilities are uneven. Mustafa Suleyman's The Coming Wave is useful on containment: the problem of getting power without surrendering control. Read together, they point toward the same practical discipline: use the capability, keep hold of the boundary.

What AI changes

Current models are good enough to matter. They draft, classify, retrieve, summarise, compare documents, call tools, and carry out narrow procedures. In a lot of office work, that changes the economics of the task.

The management problem survives the demo. A model can prepare a sales brief. It cannot decide what kind of customer the company wants. It can summarise a refund policy. It cannot decide when keeping a furious customer is worth bending the rule. It can assemble pricing evidence. It cannot decide what a price is meant to say about the company.

"Can AI do this?" is often the soft question. The harder question is where the system sits in the chain of responsibility.

Three ordinary cases

Start with account research. Before a sales call, the team wants the usual bundle: company news, funding history, product signals, executive changes, likely objections. AI is useful here. The trap is the handsome brief. Ten bullet points. Three risks. Two suggested openers. It looks like preparation. Sometimes it is merely visible information in a better suit.

The edge comes from the company's own memory: which triggers have led to revenue, which objections kill deals, which impressive-looking accounts never close. That makes account research an assisted workflow. Let the system prepare the brief. Keep the opening question and the pursuit decision with a person.

Support policy has a different shape. Many tickets are repeated, bounded, and reviewable. AI can retrieve the policy and draft the answer. The danger lives in the exception path: refunds, security, legal exposure, loyal customers on the edge of fury. This work can be automated narrowly, if escalation is designed first.

Pricing is the seductive one. The leverage is obvious. The data feels analytical. The spreadsheet glows with managerial seriousness. Yet pricing is also positioning, margin, sales motion, procurement friction, and willingness to lose certain customers. AI can collect inputs and draft scenarios. Handing it the recommendation risks hiding a strategic sacrifice inside a tidy table.

The boundary most teams blur

There are at least four separate permissions: produce an output, recommend an action, execute the action, and learn from the result. Demos make these look like one smooth path. Operations turns them back into separate risks.

Drafting a customer reply is one thing. Sending it is another. Applying a refund is another. Updating future policy because the refund "worked" is another again. The boundary is where governance begins.

Boundary Executive question Common control
Output Can AI draft, retrieve, classify, or compare safely? Source citation, formatting rules, human review.
Recommendation Can AI rank options without hiding the assumptions? Confidence bands, counter-arguments, named owner.
Execution Can AI act without creating unrecoverable harm? Approval thresholds, escalation rules, audit trail.
Learning Can the system improve without drifting from doctrine? Feedback cadence, dataset review, kill rule.

Use the label at the end

After the screen, automate / assist / ignore becomes more than a tidy taxonomy. It becomes an operating choice.

  • Automate the repeated work with a narrow boundary and a clear exception path.
  • Assist the valuable work where judgment, taste, risk, or relationship context should remain visible.
  • Ignore the work with weak context, vague ownership, costly errors, or no named decision.
Workflow Likely call Reason
Sales account research Assist It improves preparation speed, but a human still owns judgment and relationship context.
Customer support policy lookup Automate narrowly The work is repeated, bounded, reviewable, and can escalate exceptions.
Strategic pricing recommendation Ignore or assist only The consequence of a confident error is high and the context is commercially sensitive.

A quick test before the pilot

Before approving the next AI pilot, ask for one paragraph that names five things:

  • the business decision the workflow is meant to improve;
  • the person who owns the changed workflow;
  • the boundary between drafting, recommending, and acting;
  • the evidence that would count as a real improvement;
  • the condition under which the company will stop.

If the team cannot write that paragraph, it is probably too early to buy the tool. The delay may feel conservative. It is usually faster than spending a quarter discovering that the company automated a task nobody had really agreed to change.
