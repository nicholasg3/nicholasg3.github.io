The AI Workflow Investment Screen

Before buying another tool, decide what kind of work you are willing to change.

The meeting starts with a list, and the list is the tell. Sales wants its account research automated. Support has a pile of ticket summaries in mind, finance some invoice checks, product a way to boil customer interviews down to the useful parts. By now someone has seen a demo. Someone else is holding a vendor deck. And nobody at the table really wants to be the person who asks whether any of this changes what the job actually is.

Left alone, the list grows, and calling it a roadmap makes the growth look deliberate. Often what it really is turns out to be a catalogue of anxieties with software attached: tasks nobody has agreed to change, each paired with a tool that promises to do them faster.

If the workflow is not worth changing, it is not worth automating.

That line sounds like a truism until you try to act on it in a planning meeting, where it tends to make people uncomfortable. Someone now has to own the process after it changes. Someone has to review what the system puts out and catch the answer that is plausible and wrong. And some existing habit has to actually go away, or the pilot just stacks new work on top of the old work. Skip all that and the cost does not disappear. It lands on whoever inherits the workflow six months later.

Start with automate, assist, or ignore

A simple three-way frame, automate / assist / ignore, earns its keep early. Mostly it gives the room a shared vocabulary before the conversation slides into vendor names.

  • Automate when the task is narrow, repeated, reviewable, and cheap to correct.
  • Assist when the system can prepare the work while a person still owns the judgment.
  • Ignore when the process is undefined, the data is thin, no one owns it, or an error would be expensive.

As a first sort, this holds up well. As a whole strategy it is too forgiving, because almost any project can be relabeled "assist" and made to sound prudent, and nothing about the label costs you anything to apply. What it leaves out is the running cost: the review time, the managerial attention, the trust people have to extend to a draft they did not write. Knowing which of the three buckets a workflow lands in is not the same as knowing whether it earns its place there.

The first screen

Before anyone compares models, four questions are worth asking in plain language. They read as almost too simple, which is roughly why they work: they resist the kind of hedging a feature comparison invites.

  1. What decision improves? Name it specifically: which account to pursue, which ticket to escalate, which claim to make, which exception to approve.
  2. Does the work repeat? Repetition is what lets an organization compare outcomes, correct course, and learn something across instances.
  3. What does the company know that the model doesn't? If the honest answer is "nothing," the project may still save time, but it's unlikely to produce an edge.
  4. Where does responsibility stay human? The answer should be concrete enough that someone can act on it without a second meeting.

The point of the screen is to slow the room down before the demo takes over. A demo is engineered to collapse the gap between seeing something work and trusting it. A pilot has the opposite job: rebuild that gap and hold it open long enough to answer who signs off on the output, and what happens the first time the thing is confidently wrong about something that matters.

Where this argument comes from

This is an old information-systems problem in new clothing. Dale Goodhue and Ronald Thompson's research on task-technology fit made the underlying point in the 1990s: performance gains depend on how well a technology matches the task people are actually trying to do, beyond what the technology can do in isolation. The same logic transfers to generative AI without much modification. A powerful model pointed at the wrong task produces expensive theater with very little value.

Sebastian Raisch and Sebastian Krakowski add a warning that only shows up once a pilot is running, after the sales pitch is over: automation and augmentation stay entangled over time. A system introduced as an assistant can slowly absorb judgment that nobody ever formally handed it. A system built to automate can turn out to need more supervision than the deck promised. So the honest question is smaller than "replace or empower." It is really this: which specific pieces of judgment are moving out of human hands, and will anyone notice when they do.

Mohammad Hossein Jarrahi's work on human-machine symbiosis points the same direction from a different angle. Machines handle analytical processing at scale well; people carry context, tolerance for ambiguity, values, and situational judgment that doesn't reduce well to a prompt. A workflow that names this division explicitly, and then tests whether the division holds once real cases hit it, tends to outperform one that leaves the division implicit.

Recent business press has circled the same failure mode from ground level. Harvard Business Review and BetterUp use the term "workslop" for AI-generated material that looks finished while quietly pushing the interpretive work — deciding what it means, whether it's right, what to do next — onto whoever receives it. The label is inelegant, but it names a pattern a workflow screen should catch before it becomes an unremarked feature of how the office operates.

Two books are worth having on the shelf here. Ethan Mollick's Co-Intelligence is useful on treating AI as a collaborator with genuinely uneven abilities, a role that sits uneasily between tool and colleague. Mustafa Suleyman's The Coming Wave is useful on containment: the problem of gaining capability without losing the ability to govern it. Read together, they point at the same practical discipline — use the capability, and keep a hand on the boundary around it.

What AI changes

Current models are good enough to matter in ordinary office work. They can draft a document, pull the one relevant passage out of a long one, summarize a messy thread, or run a narrow procedure from start to finish. On a lot of routine tasks that genuinely changes the economics, and at this point that is not really in dispute.

What is left over after the demo is a management problem wearing a capability costume. Take a sales brief. A model will happily produce one, but the judgment about which kind of customer the company actually wants sits nowhere in that brief and never will. The same gap opens up anywhere real judgment is doing the work. A refund policy summarizes cleanly; knowing when to break it for a furious long-time customer is a different sort of act. Pricing evidence can be assembled in minutes, yet what a given price is supposed to say about the company stays a strategic choice someone has to own. Whether the tool can do the task is usually the easy question. The one that actually decides things is where the tool ends up sitting in the chain of responsibility once it is doing that task every day.

Three ordinary cases

Take account research first. Before a sales call, the team wants the familiar bundle: company news, funding history, product signals, executive changes, likely objections. AI is genuinely useful here, but the trap is the polished brief — ten bullet points, three risks, two suggested openers. It looks like preparation. Often it's public information rearranged into a more confident format.

The actual edge comes from what the company already knows and the model doesn't: which triggers have historically led to revenue, which objections reliably kill deals, which impressive-looking accounts never close. That points toward treating account research as assisted work — let the system prepare the brief, but keep the opening question and the pursuit decision with a person who carries that institutional memory.

Support policy has a different shape. Many tickets are repeated, bounded, and easy to review after the fact, which is exactly what makes a task suitable for automation. AI can retrieve the policy and draft the reply. The risk concentrates in the exception path — refunds, security, legal exposure, a loyal customer at the edge of losing patience. This work can be automated narrowly, but only if the escalation path is designed before the automation ships, not discovered after the first bad case.

Pricing is the harder one, and the one teams are most tempted to hand over. The leverage is obvious and the data looks analytical, which lends the exercise a managerial seriousness it may not have earned. Pricing is also positioning, margin structure, sales motion, procurement friction, and a set of decisions about which customers the company is willing to lose. AI can gather inputs and draft scenarios usefully. Letting it produce the recommendation risks burying a strategic tradeoff inside a table that looks like it settled the question.

The boundary most teams blur

There are at least four different permissions folded into what looks like a single feature: producing an output, recommending an action, taking the action, and learning from how it went. A demo runs them together as one smooth motion. Production pulls them apart again into four separate places where something can go wrong.

Consider a customer refund. Drafting the reply is harmless enough. Sending it commits the company's voice. Actually issuing the refund moves money out the door. And quietly adjusting the policy because that refund "worked" changes the rule for everyone next time, which happens to be the step almost nobody is watching. A sign-off at the recommendation stage is not the same thing as control once the system is executing and learning on its own, and the space between those two is exactly where governance has to live.

Boundary Executive question Common control
Output Can AI draft, retrieve, classify, or compare safely? Source citation, formatting rules, human review.
Recommendation Can AI rank options without hiding the assumptions? Confidence bands, counter-arguments, named owner.
Execution Can AI act without creating unrecoverable harm? Approval thresholds, escalation rules, audit trail.
Learning Can the system improve without drifting from doctrine? Feedback cadence, dataset review, kill rule.

Use the label at the end, not the start

After the screen has done its work, automate / assist / ignore moves from tidy taxonomy to an operating choice with consequences attached.

  • Automate the repeated work, inside a narrow boundary, with an exception path defined in advance.
  • Assist the valuable work where judgment, taste, risk, or relationship context needs to stay visible to a person.
  • Ignore the work with weak context, unclear ownership, costly failure modes, or no named decision behind it.
Workflow Likely call Reason
Sales account research Assist It improves preparation speed, but a human still owns judgment and relationship context.
Customer support policy lookup Automate narrowly The work is repeated, bounded, reviewable, and can escalate exceptions.
Strategic pricing recommendation Ignore or assist only The consequence of a confident error is high and the context is commercially sensitive.

A quick test before the pilot

Before approving the next AI pilot, ask the team to produce one paragraph naming five things:

  • the business decision the workflow is meant to improve;
  • the person who owns the changed workflow;
  • the boundary between drafting, recommending, and acting;
  • the evidence that would count as a real improvement;
  • the condition under which the company will stop.

If the team cannot write that paragraph, the tool is probably premature, whatever the demo showed. The delay feels conservative in the room, but in practice it's usually faster than spending a quarter discovering that the company automated a task nobody had actually agreed to change.
