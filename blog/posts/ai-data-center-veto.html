The AI Data-Center Veto

The next AI bottleneck may not be the model. It may be the right to draw power, use water, raise capital, win permits, and keep local voters from deciding the buildout is someone else's upside funded by their bills.

The standard executive question about AI infrastructure is still too narrow. It asks which model, which cloud, which vendor, which chip, and which workload. Those questions matter. But they assume the physical layer is available on commercial terms.

That assumption is weakening.

AI is turning data centers into visible industrial infrastructure. They need land, substations, transformers, transmission, water or advanced cooling, construction labor, long-duration contracts, and political tolerance. They do not arrive as a software subscription. They arrive as a load request.

That changes the strategy problem. A company that treats AI capacity as a procurement choice may discover that capacity is also a permission choice. The veto holder may be a utility commission, a town council, a governor, a grid operator, a water district, a financing syndicate, or a group of residents who do not believe the local bargain is fair.

The C-suite should stop treating compute as an abstract input and start treating it as a permissioned operating asset.

The Signal

The evidence now points in one direction: demand is rising quickly, but the binding constraint is shifting from chips alone to the full stack of infrastructure permission.

The International Energy Agency estimates that data centers used about 415 terawatt-hours of electricity in 2024, roughly 1.5 percent of global electricity consumption, and projects that figure to roughly double by 2030 in its base case. The IEA's more recent summary puts 2025 data-center electricity use near 485 terawatt-hours and 2030 demand near 950 terawatt-hours, with AI-focused data centers growing much faster than the total.

In the United States, the pressure is more concentrated. The Department of Energy's Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory report said U.S. data centers consumed about 4.4 percent of total U.S. electricity in 2023 and could reach 6.7 to 12 percent by 2028. EPRI's 2026 update puts the 2030 range higher: 9 to 17 percent of U.S. electricity, depending on how many projects make it through supply-chain and process constraints.

Capital is not a footnote. McKinsey estimates that global data-center demand could almost triple from 2025 to 2030, from about 82 gigawatts to about 220 gigawatts. Goldman Sachs Research says private-market financing will become more important because hyperscalers may spend roughly $5.3 trillion on AI and data centers by 2030. Another Goldman note put 2026 hyperscaler AI capex consensus at $527 billion and warned that supply bottlenecks and investor appetite may constrain spending more than balance sheets.

Then comes politics. The White House's 2026 Ratepayer Protection Pledge is a useful tell. Whatever one's politics, the managerial signal is clear: data-center growth has become a household-bill issue. The pledge asks major AI and data-center companies to build, bring, or buy new power supply, pay for power-delivery upgrades, and negotiate separate rate structures so ordinary households are not subsidizing the load. An Associated Press report this week described legislation aimed at forcing AI data centers to obtain electricity and water from private sources, and noted that more than 20 Florida counties and municipalities had rejected or delayed major data-center projects or paused them for at least a year.

This is not a local planning story. It is a strategic constraint becoming visible through local planning.

The Veto Map

Boards do not need to become utility experts. They do need a map of where the AI roadmap can be stopped.

Veto What It Blocks Executive Question
Power New load interconnection, firm capacity, backup generation, transmission upgrades. Can the AI plan name where the incremental power comes from, who pays for delivery, and what happens if grid access slips?
Permitting Site timing, zoning approvals, environmental review, construction sequencing. Which approvals are on the critical path, and is the timeline realistic compared with model and product timelines?
Water and cooling Local acceptance, design choice, operating cost, resilience in stressed regions. Has the company priced the cooling strategy as a legitimacy issue, not just an engineering issue?
Ratepayer politics Utility tariffs, cost allocation, public support, regulatory approvals. Can leaders show that households and local businesses are not underwriting a private compute strategy?
Capital discipline Build pace, balance-sheet flexibility, vendor lock-in, stranded capacity. What demand evidence justifies fixed infrastructure commitments before the AI use case fully matures?

The Strategy Error

The easy error is to call this a sustainability issue and hand it to the ESG team. That is too small. The harder and more useful frame is complementarity.

AI advantage depends on complementary assets: data rights, workflow redesign, risk controls, distribution, talent, and now physical capacity. If one complement is scarce, the value of the others changes. A better model does not help much if the inference economics are unattractive. A stronger agent roadmap does not help if production capacity is trapped behind a delayed substation. A vendor relationship does not remove the exposure if the vendor itself is competing for power, cooling equipment, construction labor, and capital.

This is why the data-center veto belongs in corporate strategy. It changes build-versus-buy decisions. It changes where workloads should run. It changes which AI use cases deserve scarce capacity. It changes procurement due diligence. It changes the definition of resilience. It changes how the company explains AI to regulators, customers, employees, and communities.

The highest-risk companies will be the ones with two incompatible beliefs: first, that AI will soon touch core operations; second, that the physical cost and permission structure behind that AI can remain invisible.

What Buyers Should Ask Vendors

Enterprise buyers do not need to interrogate every megawatt behind a vendor. But they should stop accepting capacity as magic.

Ask four questions before moving a strategic workflow onto an AI platform.

Where is the capacity assumption? If the product roadmap depends on larger models, lower latency, richer context windows, more agents, or heavier inference, the buyer should know whether the vendor can support that growth without sudden pricing, availability, or service-level changes.

Who bears infrastructure cost volatility? If power, equipment, and financing costs rise, does the vendor absorb the margin pressure, change pricing, narrow usage, or push customers toward a different architecture?

What is the fallback architecture? Critical workflows should have a plan for degraded model quality, lower-volume inference, regional capacity limits, or slower rollout. AI resilience is not only uptime. It is the ability to keep the business process functioning when compute becomes constrained.

Which claims are proven by demand, not demos? Capex can be a sign of confidence. It can also be a sign that the industry is racing ahead of monetization evidence. Buyers should separate useful capacity from speculative abundance.

The Board Agenda

The board-level version is simple: require every material AI program to name its physical dependency.

That dependency may be direct, as with a company building or leasing dedicated infrastructure. It may be indirect, as with a company whose AI platform depends on a hyperscaler's buildout. Either way, the operating plan should answer five questions.

  1. Which workflows are important enough to deserve scarce and possibly expensive AI capacity?
  2. Which vendors or infrastructure partners control the capacity path?
  3. What power, cooling, permitting, and tariff assumptions sit behind the roadmap?
  4. What happens if capacity is delayed, rationed, repriced, or politically constrained?
  5. What evidence would tell us to slow the AI buildout rather than spend through the constraint?

The last question is the most important. Infrastructure enthusiasm has a habit of turning forecasts into commitments before demand quality is proven. The answer is not to avoid AI investment. It is to stage commitments like real options: buy learning, preserve flexibility, and commit heavily only where usage evidence and operating advantage are both strong.

The Practical Move

This week, take the three AI initiatives that are most likely to become operationally important and add a one-page physical-dependency appendix to each business case.

The appendix should include the expected compute pattern, the vendor capacity assumption, cost-volatility exposure, latency or region constraints, data-residency implications, fallback mode, and the person accountable for revisiting the assumption every quarter.

That exercise will feel premature for small pilots. It is not. It is a test of seriousness. If the initiative is too small to justify an infrastructure assumption, it is probably too small to carry a transformation story. If it is big enough to matter, it is big enough to expose the company to the data-center veto.

AI strategy used to sound weightless. It now has substations, cooling systems, construction schedules, financing structures, utility tariffs, and voters attached to it.

The companies that see that first will write better AI roadmaps. The companies that do not may discover that their most important AI decision was made somewhere else.
