The AI Disclosure Clock

On 20 July the European Commission published guidelines for AI Act transparency duties that begin applying on 2 August 2026. For operators, the news is less a legal headline than a two-week deadline on which customer-facing systems must stop pretending to be silent humans.

The European Commission published guidelines on 20 July 2026 to help providers and deployers meet the AI Act's transparency obligations. Those obligations start to apply on 2 August 2026. The official aim is plain: people should be able to recognise when they are interacting with AI, or when content has been generated or altered by AI, so deception and manipulation become harder to hide behind fluency.

That is the news. The strategy question is narrower and more useful for founders, consultants, and C-suite operators: which of your live systems will look non-compliant, customer-hostile, or simply embarrassing once disclosure is expected rather than optional?

Transparency under Article 50 is becoming a workflow tax. Pay it on purpose, or pay it as an incident.

What Changed This Week

Article 50 of the EU Artificial Intelligence Act is the transparency chapter for certain interactive AI systems and AI-generated or manipulated content. The Commission's July guidelines clarify who must comply for interactive systems and for marking and labelling AI-generated content. Providers must design systems so users know when they are interacting directly with AI, and must support machine-readable marks that help detect generated or manipulated content. Deployers must inform people when they are exposed to deepfakes, to AI-generated content on matters of public interest without human review or editorial control, and to emotion recognition or biometric categorisation systems.

Two calendar facts matter more than the vocabulary. First, 2 August 2026 is close. Second, some AI Act timelines have been debated and delayed in other chapters; the public reporting around transparency and disclosure has treated Article 50 as still on the August clock. Operators who waited for a general "regulation got postponed" narrative may discover that the customer-facing disclosure duty did not wait with them.

U.S. and Asian companies are not automatically free of the issue. If the system is offered in the EU, if EU users are in scope, or if enterprise customers demand EU-grade disclosure as a commercial condition, the same checklist becomes a sales and product problem even when the legal home office is elsewhere.

Read Through Strategy Publisher Doctrine

This stack has already argued that AI strategy starts with doctrine before tools, that disclosure needs a ledger rather than a label, and that residual ownership matters when something goes wrong in public. The July guidelines force those ideas into a calendar.

Doctrine before tools. A company that only asks "which model is best this quarter?" will answer the transparency question late, with a disclaimer bolted onto a product already in market. A company with a one-page doctrine can already say which customer conversations may be AI-assisted, which public statements require human editorial control, and which content classes must carry a mark before they leave the firm.

Disclosure as workflow, not footer. Issue 019's disclosure ledger asked what was generated, where it travelled, who approved it, and when disclosure should stop publication. Article 50 makes the first half of that ledger non-optional for in-scope systems. The mark and the user notice are the public edge of an internal process: generation, review, routing, and publication.

Residual ownership. When a chatbot speaks with the brand voice and a customer later claims deception, the legal and commercial cost will not land cleanly on "the model vendor." Residual ownership sits with the deployer who put the system in front of customers. Transparency reduces some of that risk. It does not eliminate the need for a named owner of the workflow.

Four Operator Mistakes

Mistake one: treating disclosure as a marketing sentence. "Powered by AI" in a footer is not a design for interactive systems. The user needs to know, at the moment of interaction, that the counterpart is not a human agent. Product, support, and legal have to agree where that notice appears and what language is honest without being theatrical.

Mistake two: confusing public-interest content with ordinary drafts. The guidelines and surrounding commentary put weight on AI-generated content about public-interest matters when there is no human review or editorial control. A research blog, a market note, or a founder essay that is heavily machine-written without human ownership is a different risk class from an internal brainstorm. Editorial control is a control, not a vibe.

Mistake three: assuming agents are outside the story. Agentic systems that produce outputs intended to be directly perceived by users can fall into the same transparency logic. If an agent emails customers, posts content, or speaks through a channel that looks human, disclosure is part of the action boundary, not a later compliance ticket.

Mistake four: waiting for perfect specialist insurance or a perfect policy product. The coverage-gap argument from Issue 030 still applies. Regulatory disclosure and insurance residual risk are different ledgers. Neither is solved by hoping the market invents a complete product before August.

A One-Week Operator Screen

Before 2 August, score every customer-facing or public-facing AI surface against five questions.

Screen Pass signal Fail signal
Interaction notice User can tell, at first contact, that they are interacting with AI. The channel is designed to feel human and only discloses if challenged.
Content mark Public AI-generated or altered media can be labelled or machine-marked where required. Marketing and social teams can publish synthetic media with no trail.
Editorial ownership Public-interest and brand-critical content has a named human editor. Machine drafts ship because they sound finished.
Agent speech boundary Agents that message, post, or speak for the firm have an explicit disclosure rule. Agents inherit employee tone and credentials without a public identity rule.
Stop rule Missing notice or mark can pause the workflow before scale continues. Legal will "catch up after launch."

What This Digest Is For

Strategy Publisher's free stack already has doctrine, theatre tests, disclosure ledgers, control gaps, and coverage gaps. This piece is a news digest: a current market event run through that frame so an operator can decide this week, not write another abstract policy later.

The executive move is small and concrete. Inventory the five highest-blast-radius AI surfaces that talk to customers, citizens, or the public. For each, assign a disclosure owner, a notice design, a content-mark rule if media is involved, and a stop rule if those pieces are missing. If the inventory is blank, the company is not ready for a regulation that turns silence into a liability.

Model releases will keep arriving. August 2 is a different kind of product deadline. It asks whether the company can tell the truth about its machines while they speak.
