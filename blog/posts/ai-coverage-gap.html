The AI Coverage Gap

As companies put generative systems into customer, employee, and product workflows, insurers are carving AI out of ordinary liability forms. The strategic problem is not insurance trivia. It is residual risk without an owner.

Most AI programmes still treat insurance as someone else's renewal meeting. Product ships the assistant. Security debates permissions. Finance funds the pilot. Legal reviews the vendor terms. Then an output goes wrong: a false claim in a sales deck, a privacy spill through a chatbot, a defamatory summary, a hallucinated contract clause, a customer decision steered by an agent that looked confident and was wrong.

The board's first instinct is often the old industrial one: we have liability cover, cyber cover, errors-and-omissions cover. That instinct is ageing badly. In the 2026 renewal cycle, carriers and rating organisations have made generative AI an explicit exclusion surface on commercial general liability forms. Other lines are following with absolute or expansive AI carve-outs. The result is a coverage gap that looks technical on a policy endorsement and strategic on a P&L.

The AI coverage gap is the distance between the work you have automated and the losses your policies will still pay for. Strategy that ignores that distance is unfinished capital allocation.

Why This Is Not A Broker Problem Alone

Insurance markets are doing what they always do when a loss class becomes visible and hard to price: they reclassify it. Verisk's ISO commercial general liability suite now includes optional generative AI exclusion endorsements, including the broad CG 40 47 form that can remove bodily injury, property damage, and personal and advertising injury arising out of generative AI. Narrower variants target advertising injury alone or products-completed operations. The exact wording and adoption vary by carrier and state, but the direction is clear enough for operators.

Industry reporting through 2025 and 2026 has made the same point in plainer language. Carriers are excluding claims linked to generative outputs, misleading AI content, and regulatory failures tied to AI implementation. Some cyber and professional lines remain more flexible than traditional liability forms; others are tightening through security riders, unauthorized-tool exclusions, or absolute AI exclusions across D&O, E&O, and fiduciary programmes. The market is not finished. The operator cannot wait for it to finish before deciding which AI work is allowed to touch customers, money, identity, or public speech.

This is adjacent to, not a repeat of, earlier Strategy Publisher arguments. The vendor warning label concerns shared responsibility with model providers. The control-gap ledger concerns internal accountability. The evidence packet concerns buyer procurement. The coverage gap asks a different question: if the automated work fails publicly, who is holding the uninsured tail?

The Coverage Gap Has Six Lines

Operators do not need to become insurance underwriters. They need a ledger that forces one honest conversation across product, security, finance, legal, and the board.

Ledger Line Management Question Failure Mode Minimum Control
Exposure Class Which AI workflows can create a claim-facing loss? The company inventories models and vendors, not the customer-facing acts those systems perform. Map AI work by act: publish, advise, decide, transact, access data, represent the firm.
Policy Line Which insurance tower might respond: CGL, cyber, E&O, D&O, product, media? Everyone assumes "cyber will cover it" until the claim is denied as content, professional services, or excluded AI use. For each exposure class, name the intended policy line and the broker owner of that answer.
Exclusion Surface What wording already carves generative AI, synthetic content, or automated decisions out? The pilot expands while the renewal quietly attaches an absolute AI exclusion. Read current endorsements for generative AI, unauthorized tools, and silent-AI clean-up language before scale decisions.
Residual Owner If the claim is denied, who owns the residual cash, reputation, and customer cost? Product owns the feature; finance owns the policy; nobody owns the uncovered middle. Assign a named executive residual owner for each high-blast-radius AI workflow.
Control Evidence What can you prove to an underwriter, regulator, customer, or court after an incident? The company claims "human in the loop" with no logs, no approval records, and no training on forbidden uses. Keep workflow inventory, access rules, review steps, output logs, and stop rules as underwriting evidence, not theatre.
Stop Rule Which AI work must shrink or stop if coverage is absent or ambiguous? Convenience keeps a public-facing agent live while residual risk sits on the balance sheet unnoticed. Hard-stop customer advice, financial actions, identity changes, and brand speech when residual ownership is unclear.

Three Operator Mistakes

Mistake one: treating all AI as one peril. A research assistant summarizing public web pages is not the same risk as an agent that emails customers, changes CRM fields, or drafts regulatory filings. Coverage doctrine must follow the act, not the model brand.

Mistake two: confusing cyber cover with content and professional-services cover. A prompt-injection breach that exfiltrates data may look like cyber. A fluent falsehood that harms a customer may look like advertising injury, professional negligence, or media liability. If the CGL form excludes generative AI and the E&O form was written for human consultants, the claim may fall between chairs.

Mistake three: waiting for the perfect AI policy product. Specialist AI insurance will keep evolving. Some startups will fill gaps; large carriers will reprice and reword. That market development is useful, but it is not a strategy. The company still has to decide which AI work is allowed while the market sorts itself out.

What A Serious Board Should Ask

Not "do we use AI?" The better questions are operational:

Which customer-facing AI workflows can create a third-party claim this quarter? For each, which policy line is supposed to respond? Have generative AI exclusions already attached at the last renewal? Who owns residual risk if the answer is no one? What evidence would we hand an underwriter tomorrow morning? Which workflows should be demoted from act to draft until that evidence exists?

NIST's AI Risk Management Framework is useful here not as insurance advice, but as a reminder that measurement, monitoring, and governance are part of risk transfer credibility. Underwriters and counterparties increasingly ask the same questions security teams already know: what systems exist, who can act, what was reviewed, what was logged, and what can be revoked.

The Strategy Publisher Lesson

This stack has already argued that AI needs doctrine before tools, permission ledgers before autonomy, control gaps before executive theatre, and browser boundaries before credentialed agents roam free. The coverage gap is the financial twin of those operating ledgers. A company can invent clever agent workflows and still be naked if the residual loss has no policy home and no named owner.

Deep open strategy writing should keep connecting market plumbing to management choice. Insurance exclusions are market plumbing. Residual ownership is management choice. Put them on one page.

The Executive Move

This week, build a one-page AI coverage gap ledger for the five highest-blast-radius AI workflows. Fill all six lines. If residual owner or exclusion surface is blank, demote that workflow from autonomous act to assisted draft until finance, legal, and the business owner can answer in the same meeting.

Do not outsource the whole problem to the next broker renewal. Do not pretend a consumer chatbot trial is free because the software seat is cheap. The expensive part may be the uninsured claim, the customer make-good, or the board explanation that "we thought cyber covered it."

The teams that win will not be the ones with the longest AI pilot list. They will be the ones that can say, without theatre: these AI acts are covered, these are retained, these are forbidden until the residual owner and stop rules are real.
