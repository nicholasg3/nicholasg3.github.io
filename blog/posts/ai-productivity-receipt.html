The AI Productivity Receipt

AI benchmarks are improving. Productivity studies are mixed, moving, and hard to compare. The executive job is not to choose a camp. It is to demand a receipt that shows where model capability became business output.

The most dangerous sentence in AI strategy is now a mild one: "The benchmarks are getting better." It is true, important, and often useless by itself.

A benchmark can show that a model crosses a technical threshold. It cannot show that a company changed its operating system. It cannot show whether a lawyer reviewed fewer bad drafts, whether an engineer shipped more reliable code, whether a support team resolved more cases without silent escalation, or whether a founder won back time that was previously lost to coordination. The benchmark is a capability signal. The business still needs a receipt.

This distinction matters because the evidence picture has become more complicated, not less. Stanford HAI's 2025 AI Index records sharp gains on demanding benchmarks and rising business adoption. OpenAI first helped popularise SWE-bench Verified as a better test of real software tasks, then in 2026 argued that the benchmark no longer measures frontier coding capability well enough and pointed users toward SWE-bench Pro. METR's early-2025 randomized study found experienced open-source developers took longer with AI tools, then its 2026 update said newer measurement was biased because many developers no longer wanted to work without AI. Management Science published field experiments across Microsoft, Accenture, and a Fortune 100 company finding a meaningful increase in completed developer tasks, with larger gains for less experienced developers.

The headline is not contradiction. The headline is accounting. AI can be technically stronger, economically valuable in some contexts, distracting in others, and very hard to measure with one universal yardstick. That is exactly why executives need a productivity receipt.

Benchmark progress is not business progress until the company can show the task changed, the workflow absorbed it, the economics improved, and the control burden stayed tolerable.

The Benchmark Trap

The benchmark trap is not believing benchmarks. It is overextending them.

Benchmarks are indispensable for model builders and useful for buyers. They create a common scoreboard, expose capability direction, and help separate serious systems from vapor. A coding benchmark can indicate whether an agent can repair repository issues. A reasoning benchmark can indicate whether a model handles harder formal tasks. A multimodal benchmark can show whether a model can parse richer inputs. These are real signals.

But a benchmark is usually cleaner than a company. The task is bounded. The answer can be scored. The environment is controlled. The incentive is to maximize performance against a known test. Business work is messier. Requirements arrive half-formed. The answer can be socially correct but commercially wrong. The process changes when humans know a tool is available. The cost may move from production time to review time, integration time, exception handling, data cleanup, or management oversight.

That is why benchmark gains so often create boardroom overconfidence. They compress the messy question - "What changed in our operating model?" - into a cleaner one: "Did the model score improve?"

The Study Trap

The mirror-image mistake is to use one productivity study as a veto over the whole field.

METR's experienced-developer study was valuable precisely because it cut against easy hype. It studied real open-source tasks in familiar repositories and found slowdown in that setting. The obvious managerial lesson is not "AI does not work." It is that AI productivity depends on task selection, user skill, repository context, tool maturity, and measurement design.

The later METR update makes the point stronger. When AI tools become part of developers' normal way of working, randomized measurement becomes harder. Developers may resist no-AI conditions. Some may run multiple agents at once. Self-reported time becomes less reliable. The treatment changes the labor market being measured. That is not a footnote. It is the product.

The Management Science field experiments point in the other direction: across thousands of developers and enterprise contexts, AI coding assistance increased completed tasks on average, with less experienced developers benefiting more. That finding is commercially important. It still does not eliminate the need for local measurement. A founder cannot deposit an average effect size into their own P&L.

The Receipt

A useful AI productivity receipt has five lines.

Receipt Line Question Bad Substitute
Benchmark delta What external capability signal changed, and does it map to our task family? A vendor slide with a leaderboard number and no task fit.
Task delta What specific unit of work became faster, cheaper, better, or newly possible? "People feel more productive" without observed output.
Workflow delta Which handoff, review, approval, or exception path changed because of the tool? A faster draft that still waits in the same queue.
Economic delta What cost, revenue, cycle-time, risk, or capacity metric improved after all new costs? Minutes saved before counting review, integration, license, and support burden.
Trust delta Did the system remain auditable, reversible, and acceptable to the people who own the outcome? More automation with no owner for failures.

These lines force a company to keep the technical claim and the operating claim separate. The benchmark delta asks whether model capability is relevant. The task delta asks whether the worker's unit of output changed. The workflow delta asks whether the organization absorbed the change. The economic delta asks whether the improvement survived full cost accounting. The trust delta asks whether the company can live with the new failure mode.

Why Managers Miss It

Managers miss the receipt because AI often produces visible artifacts before it produces measurable advantage. A draft appears. A summary appears. A patch appears. A spreadsheet formula appears. These outputs feel like progress because the old blank page has disappeared. But a business is not paid for the disappearance of blank pages. It is paid for decisions, shipped work, risk reduction, customer trust, and capacity.

This is why the same tool can produce both enthusiasm and disappointment inside one company. A junior analyst may get a real lift because the model scaffolds first drafts and teaches patterns. A senior engineer in a complex repository may slow down because review and correction exceed generation gains. A support team may close easy cases faster while accidentally moving hard cases to a smaller group of overloaded experts. A founder may write more memos while making fewer decisions.

The problem is not the tool. The problem is lazy aggregation. "AI productivity" is too broad a bucket. It hides the difference between task assistance, skill transfer, workflow compression, quality improvement, capacity release, and strategic substitution.

The CFO Question

The CFO should not ask whether the company is "using AI." That question is now too cheap. The CFO should ask which receipt line is expected to move and what evidence will count.

For a coding assistant, the economic delta may be merged pull requests, escaped defects, review time, cycle time, or onboarding speed. For a sales assistant, it may be qualified follow-ups, better account research, higher meeting quality, or lower rep admin time. For a strategy desk, it may be faster source digestion, better hypothesis generation, or higher-quality partner conversations. For a customer-support agent, it may be containment rate adjusted for escalation quality and customer trust.

The critical phrase is "adjusted for." Productivity claims that ignore adjustment are usually theatre. Count the output, then count the burden: licenses, prompt and workflow design, data governance, review, rework, audit, incident response, employee training, customer disclosure, and managerial attention. The net figure is the receipt. The gross figure is advertising.

The Founder Version

Founders should use a smaller version of the same receipt. They rarely have enough data for formal experiments. They do have enough discipline to define a before-and-after operating test.

Pick one repeated workflow. Define the current baseline in plain units: hours per memo, days from customer question to answer, number of sales accounts researched per week, bug-fix cycle time, support tickets resolved without founder involvement, or qualified article ideas turned into publishable drafts. Then run the AI-assisted version long enough to observe the hidden costs. Do not count the first dazzling session. Count the third, fifth, and tenth pass, when novelty has worn off and edge cases appear.

The founder should then write one paragraph: "We will keep this tool in this workflow because it changes this unit of work, at this cost, with this owner, under this stop rule." If that paragraph cannot be written, the company is still experimenting. That is fine. It is not yet strategy.

The Executive Test

Before approving the next AI rollout, require five answers.

  1. Which benchmark or external capability signal justifies testing this now?
  2. Which internal task family is close enough to that signal to make the test meaningful?
  3. Which workflow handoff changes if the tool works?
  4. Which economic measure will improve after full operating costs are counted?
  5. Who can stop or roll back the system if the trust delta is negative?

A vendor can help answer the first question. Only the buyer can answer the other four. That is the boundary executives should defend.

The Better Argument

The mature AI argument is no longer "AI is overhyped" or "AI changes everything." Both are too imprecise for management.

The better argument is that AI capability is advancing fast enough to require repeated local tests, and uneven enough to punish companies that skip accounting. Benchmarks tell executives where to look. Field studies tell them which effects are plausible. Their own receipts tell them whether the operating model changed.

A company that keeps receipts can invest aggressively without becoming credulous. A company that does not keep receipts will alternate between hype and backlash, mistaking each mood swing for strategy.
