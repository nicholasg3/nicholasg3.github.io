The AI Herding Problem

When every team can ask similar models similar questions, the scarce asset is no longer analysis. It is independent judgment about which answers should be trusted, ignored, or deliberately opposed.

The quiet risk in enterprise AI is not that the machine will be too strange. It is that it will be too familiar.

Give a dozen strategy teams the same market facts, the same prompt templates, the same vendor dashboards, and access to similar frontier models. The output will not be identical, but it will often rhyme. The recommended markets will look adjacent. The risk lists will share the same shape. The customer segments will be named in the same language. The board decks will appear sharper while the underlying judgment becomes more correlated.

That is the AI herding problem.

Executives have spent the last two years asking whether AI can make knowledge work faster. The better question is what happens after it does. If analysis becomes cheaper and more evenly distributed, advantage shifts toward the people and institutions that know when not to follow the machine-mediated consensus.

The strategic question is not whether AI improves analysis. It is whether it makes your judgment more independent or more crowded.

The Market Clue

Goldman Sachs Exchanges recently asked whether AI could make markets less efficient. The investing argument is useful even for people who never buy a stock. If many participants use broadly similar models, the technology can improve discovery in under-covered areas while also creating crowding where the models produce similar answers for similar users.

Translated into operating language: AI may make routine analysis better and strategic behavior more imitative at the same time.

This is not a contradiction. Models can summarize more evidence, generate more options, and surface more anomalies than a tired team working alone. They can also compress language, normalize assumptions, and give executives a dangerous feeling that a recommendation has been independently tested because it looks polished. The analysis improves locally while the organization's decision ecology becomes more uniform.

That risk matters because AI adoption is becoming broad before AI management is becoming mature. McKinsey's 2025 State of AI work found that companies are starting to redesign workflows, elevate governance, and mitigate more risks, but meaningful bottom-line impact remains uneven. BCG's 2026 research found that only a small share of companies qualify as AI leaders, and that leaders are separated by talent, workflow change, reinvestment, and business performance rather than talk about tools. Deloitte's enterprise research shows the same friction from another angle: many generative AI experiments still struggle to scale even as enthusiasm and budgets continue.

In other words, the market is moving toward universal access before most firms have built a doctrine for independent use.

How Herding Enters The Company

Herding rarely announces itself as herding. It arrives as sensible process improvement.

The first path is template herding. A central team publishes prompt libraries, use-case canvases, scoring rubrics, risk taxonomies, and board-slide formats. This is useful. It also pushes messy strategic questions into the same conceptual mold. Over time, unusual but important local facts start to look like exceptions to the template rather than inputs that should change the answer.

The second path is vendor herding. Enterprise platforms increasingly package not just tools, but assumptions about how sales, support, software development, finance, legal review, and customer operations should work. Buyers adopt the software and inherit a management theory. If rivals buy the same theory, differentiation has to come from the surrounding operating model, not the workflow label.

The third path is benchmark herding. Leaders ask what peers are doing because the board asks what peers are doing. Consultants package cross-client patterns. Analysts reward visible AI activity. The company that wanted an advantage starts optimizing for recognizability.

The fourth path is confidence herding. AI output often arrives with fluent structure: pros, cons, scenarios, implementation steps, and risks. That structure can hide weak premises. A human recommendation with visible uncertainty may lose to a model-assisted recommendation with smoother prose, even when the human noticed the thing that matters.

The Judgment Premium

The answer is not to slow adoption. It is to raise the value of human judgment at the points where correlated analysis is most dangerous.

NIST's AI Risk Management Framework and its generative AI profile are useful here because they push organizations toward governed, mapped, measured, and managed systems rather than loose experimentation. But governance is not enough if it becomes paperwork around the same herd. The missing layer is decision design: deciding in advance which choices should be AI-assisted, which should be AI-challenged, and which should remain explicitly human-owned.

Human-owned does not mean human-only. It means the final judgment has a named accountable owner, a record of dissenting evidence, and a reason for accepting or rejecting the machine-supported consensus.

There are five decisions where that standard should be high.

Decision Why AI Herding Is Dangerous Human-Owned Test
Market entry Models overweight public evidence and common category language, which can make crowded markets look safer than neglected ones. What proprietary reason do we have to win that a model using public information would not see?
Customer promise AI can optimize messaging toward familiar benefits and erase the awkward promise that actually makes the business distinct. Which customer truth are we willing to state more sharply than competitors?
Risk appetite Standardized risk registers can make rare, local, or reputational risks look secondary because they do not fit the common taxonomy. What failure would be uniquely damaging to us even if it scores low in a generic model?
Capital allocation Model-assisted business cases can converge on the same productivity assumptions, payback periods, and vendor narratives. What evidence would make us stop funding this even if peers keep spending?
Operating doctrine Tools can standardize how work is described before leaders decide what work should become more human, more automated, or more controlled. Which decisions must remain accountable to a person because trust, legitimacy, or strategy depends on it?

The Useful Friction

Good AI management should create friction in a few places. Not bureaucratic drag. Useful friction.

Before a major AI-assisted recommendation reaches an executive committee, require a dissent pass. Ask one person to use the same evidence to argue the opposite conclusion. Ask another to identify what the model could not know because the fact is private, political, tacit, or too new. Ask finance to separate productivity claims from actual cash impact. Ask the workflow owner what will change on Monday if the recommendation is approved.

This sounds slower. It is often faster than approving a polished consensus and discovering later that nobody owned the assumption that mattered.

IBM's 2025 CEO study is a useful warning because it shows leaders pushing harder into AI while also naming disconnected technology, data architecture, funding balance, incentives, and expertise as live constraints. The problem is not lack of ambition. The problem is that ambition without decision discipline becomes expensive conformity.

Where AI Should Disagree

A mature AI organization should not only ask models for answers. It should ask them for structured disagreement.

Use one model pass to summarize the strongest case for action. Use another to build the case against action. Use a third to identify what would need to be true for the recommendation to fail. Then make the accountable human owner write the final decision in plain language.

The goal is not to manufacture contrarianism. It is to prevent the company from outsourcing the shape of its thinking. A decision process that cannot explain why it rejected a plausible AI-generated answer is not stronger than the model. A decision process that accepts the answer without naming its own proprietary insight is not strategy.

The Practical Move

This week, take the three most important AI-assisted decisions currently moving through the company. For each one, add a one-page herding check:

  1. Which parts of this recommendation could any competitor generate with the same public facts?
  2. Which assumptions came from vendor material, benchmark language, or standard prompt templates?
  3. What private fact, customer context, operational constraint, or founder judgment changes the answer?
  4. What is the strongest case against the recommendation?
  5. Who owns the final judgment, and what evidence would make that person reverse course?

The companies that win with AI will not be the ones that avoid models. They will be the ones that use models without becoming model-shaped. They will get the speed, breadth, and memory of AI while protecting the human judgment that makes a business different from its peers.

When analysis becomes cheap, independent judgment becomes expensive. That is where the next advantage will sit.
